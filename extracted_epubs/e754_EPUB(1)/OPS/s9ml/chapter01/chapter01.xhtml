Financial Metrics at DelishGo

Having raised $150 million in a funding round in 2023 on a $1 billion post-money valuation, DelishGo experienced rapid growth. A few months earlier, the business was burning just a few million dollars a month. However, priorities shifted from validating the business model to expanding into new geographical markets, ramping up marketing efforts and ensuring premium customer service. As a result, DelishGo’s unit economics worsened, and its burn rate increased to over $10 million per month.

Thanks to the recent funding round and investors’ continuing enthusiasm for the Los Angeles-based unicorn, cash was not an immediate concern provided the company could quickly improve its unit economics. With a Board meeting coming up to conclude 2023, founder and CEO Kelly Rivera, along with CFO Chris Kim and the senior team, recognized the need for metrics that clearly represented the company’s current status and also communicated and guided DelishGo’s journey to financial success in the not-too-distant future.

Business Model

Founded in 2019, DelishGo created an online marketplace where customers pay drivers to deliver groceries and other products from local stores. Customers submitted their orders and paid online or through a mobile app. Upon order submission, drivers would receive, fulfill, and deliver the customers’ requests. As with many marketplaces, balancing supply and demand presented a constant challenge. “Internally at DelishGo, we view this as fundamentally a machine learning challenge,” Kim explained. “Our system constantly operates models to forecast the required number of delivery personnel at any given moment, in any location.” With its technology-driven approach, DelishGo facilitated delivery in as little as an hour.

DelishGo’s multi-sided marketplace created an ecosystem that was complex and quite different from the traditional customer-supplier relationships. Instead, DelishGo had direct relationships with retailers, customers, drivers, and consumer product companies.

  • DelishGo contracted with retailers like grocery stores, pharmacies, and convenience stores. In exchange for fulfilling and delivering orders from these retailers to customers, DelishGo charged retailers a fee that was generally a percentage value of the order.

  • DelishGo customers paid delivery fees and a service fee, which depended on the time, location, and size of the order. DelishGo also offered “DelishGo Pro,” an annual membership priced at $100 that provided members with free delivery and reduced service fees.

  • DelishGo drivers were independent contractors who shopped and delivered orders if and when customers placed orders online. Driver payouts were calculated on a variety of factors including the size of the order, estimated delivery distance, and time of day when the order was placed.

  • DelishGo monetized consumer product companies like Pepsi and Coca Cola who advertised on the app by charging for display ads, product placement and coupon redemptions.

Key Factor for Success: Scale

For DelishGo, as was true for most platform start-ups, getting to scale was critical. Only when the order volume in an area was sufficiently large were drivers able to fulfill the number of orders that allowed them to earn a compelling amount from fees. The business’s profitability also hinged on achieving high order volumes to generate sufficient gross margin. Although DelishGo’s service was first available in Los Angeles, it quickly expanded to dozens of cities to increase its scale.

Scale also played an important role in the success of DelishGo’s advertising business. The larger the customer reach and the more products DelishGo sold, the more attractive it would be for consumer product companies to promote on the platform.

While the Board fully understood the importance of economies of scale, Kim feared they might be caught off guard by the high burn and the significantly negative gross margins of the business. As he prepared for the board meeting, he recognized the importance of providing a clear and concise explanation of DelishGo’s economic situation.

Unit Economics

In its early days, DelishGo had mostly focused on measuring gross margin as a measure of unit economics for its short-term goals. In preparation for the board meeting, Kim and his colleagues had developed a plan for gross margin to turn positive (Exhibit 1).

However, even if they met that goal, his focus would need to shift toward defining and improving contribution margin, which would offer a more comprehensive view on the true unit economics of the business. Achieving positive gross and contribution margins required a thorough understanding of the unit economics and a vision and strategy for turning it positive.

Gross Margins

DelishGo’s gross margin was simply defined as its revenue less its costs of revenue. Revenue included all fees from retailers, customers, and consumer goods companies. Its costs of revenue included fulfillment costs such as driver earnings, credit card processing fees, and hosting costs. DelishGo analyzed the gross profit per order to determine the amount of cash each order generated for the business.

Other Costs

DelishGo’s additional expenditures fell into several key categories: operations and support, highlighted by expenses for drivers’ support hotlines, wages for customer support staff, cost of attracting new drivers, and development of new driver onboarding modules; sales and marketing, which included investments in digital advertising across social media, search engines and national brand advertising; research and development, covering engineering staff salaries and software licensing fees; and general and administrative costs, encompassed by background check fees for drivers, accounting services, wages of human resource employees, third-party consulting, and other overhead expenses.

DelishGo’s leadership team would need to debate the timing and necessity of including additional costs such as operations and support or customer marketing costs in the calculation of contribution margin. Of course, including more costs in the calculations would make its unit economics look worse (Exhibit 2, 3). How would investors react?

GAAP vs. Non-GAAP Financials

As with all private companies, DelishGo was free to manage its own books however it wished. To date, while DelishGo had published audited financials under generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP), management relied on its own internal key performance indicators when making management decisions and talking to investors, most notably focusing on adjusted earnings before interest, depreciation and amortization (EBITDA) instead of GAAP net income.

The difference between the two profit and loss statements could be significant; for example, U.S. GAAP included estimates of the costs of stock-based compensation, reserves for legal and tax matters, acquisition related costs, etc. whereas DelishGo’s Adjusted EBITDA excluded these costs (Exhibit 4). Public company peers like Lyft and Doordash added back these costs in their Adjusted EBITDA definition, but they could add up to hundreds of millions of dollars (Exhibit 5, 6). Was it reasonable for DelishGo and its public peers to focus investors on Adjusted EBITDA?

Kim, Rivera, and other members of the senior team had a lot to think about—which financial metrics would provide the board and investors with an accurate snapshot of where DelishGo was at that very moment, as well as a clear sense of its growth trajectory?
