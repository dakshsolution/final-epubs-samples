“People worked very hard on this property deal and I would hate them to think the board didn’t appreciate their efforts.” That was how Lucas Bass, CEO of NLInvest, started his speech at the emergency board meeting that was called to discuss a potentially serious governance failure linked to the purchase of a new building on a technology campus.

The chair of the board, Maarten Jansen, having been urged by Lucas, who was “travelling”, to sign further documentation regarding the purchase, had subsequently discovered that his predecessor, Janice de Vries, had similarly been asked to sign a purchase contract without discussion by or approval from a full board meeting. After taking independent legal advice, Maarten was convinced that he could not legitimately sign. When challenged, Lucas had assured him that the agency’s lawyers had confirmed Janice had the right to sign.

Before the meeting, Maarten called the non-executive directors to ascertain their views on the seriousness of the issue, and more importantly, whether Lucas should be asked to resign.

Preparing for the Showdown

As Maarten considered his strategy before the board meeting, he realised how furious he was with Lucas for creating a serious governance problem for the agency, and trying to manoeuvre him into making a similar mistake. Moreover, he had seen no sign of the legal advice that Lucas assured him would confirm the legitimacy of the procedure. Trying not to take it personally, he told himself to focus not on achieving what he would consider a good outcome, but on ensuring a fair and effective process.

He was clear in his mind that the priority was to put in place the steps required to sort out the property purchase documentation and approval. This was the most pressing issue and the area of greatest risk, hence must take precedence over any consideration of the CEO’s future. He worked with his legal advisers to prepare a script to ensure the minutes would regularise the governance arrangements.

While admitting his antipathy towards Lucas, which he guessed was shared by at least some of the board, he realised that it might lead to an unstructured or unfair process when deciding the fate of the CEO. As chair, he had to frame the issues carefully, avoid an emotional response or taking unnecessarily radical steps, while ensuring the board carried out its fiduciary duty.

As always, he jotted down what he would say at the meeting so as to stay on track should there be any provocation by Lucas or an emotional response from board members. He was determined not to arrive with his mind already made up about the board’s decision. He would use the non-executive session at the beginning of the meeting to frame the problem, ensuring adequate time for the directors to calmly discuss potential actions and mitigation strategies.

Taking the Temperature

Before the meeting, Maarten reached out to some board members (Exhibit 1) to get a feel for the potential dynamics and to achieve consensus. He wasn’t surprised that Frances Brower, who had regularly railed against senior management for their lack of respect, thought Lucas should be fired. Several board members were of the view that in the private sector Lucas would be fired, but given the complications of removing people from office in the public sector, any such decision would be highly disruptive for the agency and its staff. Two other members did not see the offence as sufficiently problematic to overrule Lucas’s strong performance record.

Maarten was particularly keen to hear the view of Bart Hendriks, chair of the Audit and Risk Committee (ARC), a highly-respected director. He knew Bart would have a significant influence on the outcome of the board meeting. As an executive himself, Bart had been sympathetic to letting Lucas run the show, but had become increasingly disillusioned with Lucas’s apparent lack of interest in day-to-day operations as the CEO increasingly focused on securing a new role. Bart told Maarten he was embarrassed he had not spotted the procedural error made in acquiring the new building. He was furious about Lucas’s treatment of Janice, who he considered a good friend. But ultimately, with Lucas having no more than a year before the end of his contract and his view of no obvious successor, he concluded that didn’t make sense to destabilise the agency by rendering it leaderless.

The Board Meeting

Reading from his notes, Maarten set out his version of events: how he had been asked to sign the lease for a parking lot and why he had refused to do so. He explained how, in taking legal advice on the issue, he discovered that Janice had signed the building purchase contracts without the appropriate board approval process and that it was his understanding that senior management had advised her to do so.

One board member, clearly discontented with the situation, asked whether Janice was aware of what had happened. Lucas responded that the senior management had put an “extraordinary amount of effort” into the property purchase, that his team were “stressed out”, and the board should think carefully as “this sort of criticism might push them over the edge”.

Struggling to remain calm, Maarten retorted that their “effort” was not an excuse for putting the board in a parlous situation, and that it was Lucas who was accountable for the governance failure – not his team. Trying to control himself, he lowered his voice and calmly told Lucas that the consequences of the governance failure would be discussed later; the current agenda item was about correcting the problem. Lucas fell silent.

With no further contributions from directors at that point, Maarten read a resolution – carefully crafted by his lawyers – which proposed retrospective approval of the property acquisition by the board and authorised the CEO to sign the lease for the parking lot. There were no questions or objections from the directors and the resolution was approved.

Then Maarten asked Lucas to leave the meeting. Lucas protested, arguing that as a board member he had a right and an obligation to be present. But Emily Peters, who was known to be close to Lucas, cut him off, saying, “Lucas, for your own sake, you should not be here for this discussion.” Looking chastened, he nodded to her and left the room.

Maarten then invited the directors to give their views on Lucas’ accountability and the potential consequences. One director said that that trust had been seriously damaged and wondered how the board could be confident they would not be put in a similar position again. Several others stated that they were concerned that senior management did not understand how serious the matter was, and one insisted, “We have to make it clear that any further governance failures would result in termination of the CEO’s contract.” Another spoke highly of Lucas and asked the board to remind themselves what had been achieved over the course of his tenure.

Bart Hendriks, chair of the Audit Committee, said Lucas had been “lucky”, adding, “Ironically, the hard work of the chair in obtaining independent legal advice has resulted in what appears to be a rather simple fix to tidy up the mess made by Lucas. However, just because we caught it and found a way to regularise it, we must not forget this was a major failure of governance caused by a disrespectful CEO playing with the rules.”

When Maarten asked Bart as to whether the implication of that was that the CEO should resign, Bart answered that Lucas enjoyed strong support in the agency, that there was no obvious successor, and he did not favour termination. Emily Peters agreed. Bart insisted, however, that Lucas had to change his attitude and respect the board as the highest decision-making body and his boss. He recommended a governance review (Exhibits 2 and 3). Maarten asked the directors if they would support that proposal, and they agreed.

After the in camera session, Maarten asked Lucas to return. The chair summarised the discussion and communicated the decision they had made to an uncharacteristically quiet Lucas. He looked shocked, then relieved. Maarten also informed Lucas that in anticipation of the end of his contract, the board would begin the process of recruiting a new CEO.

The Long Goodbye

“For God’s sake, or at least for the sake of NLInvest, hire a CEO who knows how to work with a board.” Bart Hendriks imparted this advice to his chuckling fellow board members sitting in camera, as they wrapped up a discussion item on the establishment of a CEO selection committee to handle the search for a new CEO. The committee consisted of Maarten as chair, Ann Bakker and Caroline Vos. Both Caroline and Ann had served for four years on the board, so were readily accepted by the other members as being suitably qualified and experienced. Maarten had proposed them as they had been the most articulate of the need for a change in culture at the agency.

Page 4

The committee’s tasks, as mandated by the board, were to:

  1. Arrange a search and selection process, which would present two qualified candidates to the board, from which one would be appointed CEO;

  2. Recommend the process for onboarding the new CEO, and for managing his/her progress over the first year.

CEO Succession

Despite the many occasions when Lucas had indicated that he was likely to be headhunted before his departure, he stayed until the very end of his contract. However, the momentum changed after the emergency board meeting. A change in the attitude of the non-executive directors enabled Maarten to garner their support for his reforms. The level of trust and camaraderie between board members increased, and board meetings became a forum for debate with a high level of engagement.

Maarten commissioned an external board evaluation which confirmed positive change both in directors’ attitudes and board practices, and the governance review resulted in the writing of a new board manual. However, the culture of the senior management team remained a problem, one which a majority of board members privately admitted would not change until a new CEO was appointed.

The succession committee met formally six times and regularly communicated via email. At their first meeting they defined the selection criteria and the terms and conditions of the job (Exhibit 4). With the assistance of the agency’s HR unit, they ran a procurement exercise to appoint an international search agency and subsequently agree on the final details of the job profile information and advertising strategy. The committee met with the search agency to discuss a longlist of candidates and selected seven for a first interview. A second round of interviews with four candidates took place several weeks later. Two candidates (Exhibit 5 and 6) were selected for a final interview.

The committee engaged a specialised consulting company to run an assessment of the two candidates and provide a ‘personality inventory’. After the final round of interviews, the committee passed both files on to the board members for final discussion (Exhibit 5 and 6). Both candidates had indicated that if offered the role, they would accept the terms and conditions offered. The position, for an initial five-year term with the possibility of renewal, carried a non-negotiable (in accordance with public sector rules) salary of €265,000, and moving expenses.

The committee also prepared recommendations regarding the onboarding and evaluation of the new CEO. While unusual for such a position, the board accepted the committee’s view that given the need for a change of culture in the organisation, the probation and evaluation of the CEO should be no different to that of any other staff member – a recommended minimum of nine months, with reviews at three-monthly intervals carried out by a three-person sub-committee of the Management Development and Remuneration Committee (MDRC), who would make their recommendation to the board at the end of the probation period. It was agreed that the committee would be chaired by Maarten.

A New Dawn?

Maarten was content with the shortlist the committee had drawn up. As he readied himself for the board meeting, he had his own idea about who would best lead the agency in the next five years – he had a clear favourite. He was less clear who the board would pick. Whoever the successful candidate, it couldn’t be as bad as working with Lucas .... could it?

  • The Board has collective responsibility to establish the strategic direction of the Agency.

  • Within the resources allocated to it by government, the Board approves major strategic initiatives and programmes.

  • The Board sets the ethical tone of the Agency, not only by its own actions but also in overseeing senior management and staff, to ensure good standards of governance and ethical behaviour within the Agency.

  • The Board ensures that the Agency complies with all statutory and administrative requirements for the use of public funds.

  • The Board is required to assure itself that the Agency’s systems of internal financial control, risk management, and operations compliance are appropriate.

  • Board Members sign a conflict-of-interest policy and complete an annual declaration of interests.

  • Board Members, in the furtherance of their duties, may take independent professional advice, if necessary, at the reasonable expense of the Agency, and approved by the Chair.

Exhibit 4

Selection Criteria for CEO Role

As Chief Executive Officer of NLInvest, you will lead the Agency in securing investment in the Netherlands from the world’s leading companies by creating a world-leading investment environment for top-tier companies, in strategic sectors, providing employment and prosperity for the people of the Netherlands. Reporting to the Board, the CEO will bring strategic vision and leadership to the agency to achieve its ambitious strategy.

Selection Criteria:

  • Proven and extensive track record of attainment and achievement at senior leadership level with responsibility for significant employee and financial resources.

  • Deep understanding of the international business scene, market dynamics, and the sectors in which NLInvest sources investment.

  • Senior experience of leading, with agility and resilience, complex strategic and change management initiatives.

  • Confidence and decisiveness, as well as having the self-awareness to understand one’s strengths and limitations, who can delegate successfully, and engage in inclusive decision-making.

  • Sound judgment and an ability to manage difficult and complex issues in a dynamic environment.

  • Strong commitment to excellent corporate governance and experience of reporting to, and successfully working with, a non-executive board.

  • Strong advocacy and presentational skills, and a proven ability to influence.

  • Relevant degree-level qualification desirable.
